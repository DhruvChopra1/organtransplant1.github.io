About Organ Transplant Technology

Understanding the science and process that has saved millions of lives worldwide

The Science of Giving Life

Organ transplantation is one of the greatest achievements in modern medicine, allowing doctors to replace failing organs with healthy ones from donors. Learn more about this life-saving procedure below.

1

What is an Organ Transplant?

An organ transplant is a surgical procedure where a failing or damaged organ is removed and replaced with a functioning one from a donor. This life-saving procedure is performed for organs that are vital for survival when they've been damaged beyond repair by disease or injury. Commonly transplanted organs include kidneys, liver, heart, lungs, pancreas, and intestines.

2

The Transplantation Process

Successful transplantation requires careful matching between donor and recipient. Doctors evaluate blood type, tissue compatibility, organ size, medical urgency, and waiting time before determining priority. Once a match is found, the organ is carefully preserved and transported to the recipient's location, where surgeons perform the intricate procedure to connect the new organ to the body's systems.

Understand organ matching →
3

How It Works

The transplant procedure involves carefully removing the damaged organ while preserving vital connections to blood vessels and other tissues. Surgeons then implant the donor organ, connecting blood vessels and other necessary structures. Once these connections are established, blood flow is restored to the new organ, allowing it to begin functioning in its new body. The entire procedure requires extraordinary precision and expertise.

4

Post-Transplant Care

After surgery, patients undergo intensive monitoring in a specialized unit. Immunosuppressant medications are given to prevent the body from rejecting the new organ. Recipients must adhere to a strict medication schedule, attend regular check-ups, and follow specific lifestyle guidelines. This careful management is essential for the transplanted organ to continue functioning properly and for the patient to live a healthy life.

5

The Impact

Organ transplantation has revolutionized medicine and saved countless lives. In 2022 alone, over 40,000 organ transplants were performed in the USA, giving recipients a second chance at life. Beyond survival, transplants dramatically improve quality of life, allowing recipients to return to work, family responsibilities, and activities they enjoy. Each successful transplant represents both medical achievement and the profound generosity of donors and their families.

Types of Organ Transplants

Each type of organ transplant presents unique challenges and benefits. Explore the different procedures below to learn about these life-saving operations.

Kidney Transplant

Most common type of organ transplant performed worldwide.

Kidney Transplant

Success Rate: 90-95% one-year survival rate

Waiting List: Approximately 90,000 people

Living Donors: Possible (a person can live with just one kidney)

Kidney transplants are performed to replace kidneys damaged by conditions like diabetes, high blood pressure, and polycystic kidney disease. The procedure typically takes 3-4 hours.

Liver Transplant

Second most common transplant, with partial donation possible.

Liver Transplant

Success Rate: 75-85% five-year survival rate

Waiting List: Around 11,000 people

Living Donors: Possible (partial liver donation)

Liver transplants treat end-stage liver disease and acute liver failure. The liver's unique ability to regenerate allows for living donation where a portion of a healthy liver can be transplanted.

Heart Transplant

Life-saving procedure for end-stage heart failure.

Heart Transplant

Success Rate: 85-90% one-year survival rate

Waiting List: About 3,500 people

Living Donors: Not possible

Heart transplants are performed for patients with severe heart failure who have not responded to other treatments. The procedure is complex and requires careful matching of donor size and blood type.

Lung Transplant

Can involve one or both lungs depending on the condition.

Lung Transplant

Success Rate: 80-85% one-year survival rate

Waiting List: Around 1,000 people

Living Donors: Possible (lobar donation)

Lung transplants treat advanced lung diseases like cystic fibrosis, COPD, and pulmonary fibrosis. Single lung, double lung, or heart-lung transplants may be performed depending on the patient's condition.

Pancreas Transplant

Often performed alongside kidney transplants for diabetic patients.

Pancreas Transplant

Success Rate: 75-80% one-year graft survival

Waiting List: Approximately 1,200 people

Living Donors: Not typically possible

Pancreas transplants are primarily performed for type 1 diabetes patients with kidney failure. The procedure can free patients from insulin dependency and prevent further diabetic complications.

Intestinal Transplant

Rare and complex procedure for intestinal failure.

Intestinal Transplant

Success Rate: 65-70% one-year survival rate

Waiting List: Around 250 people

Living Donors: Not possible

Intestinal transplants are performed for patients with intestinal failure who cannot absorb nutrients and require total parenteral nutrition (TPN). This complex procedure may include transplantation of multiple organs.
